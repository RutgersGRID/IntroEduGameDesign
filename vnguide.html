A Faculty Guide to Creating Visual Novels

Harnessing Interactive Storytelling for Education

Intro to Visual Novels & Their Educational Roots

A Visual Novel (VN) is a form of interactive fiction that combines a textual story with static or semi-static visuals, background music, and sound effects. While often associated with Japanese entertainment, the core concept—a story that unfolds based on player choice—has deep roots in education and early computer-based learning. Early forms of interactive narratives, like the PLATO system in the 1960s, were used to create educational simulations and "Choose Your Own Adventure" style learning modules, allowing students to explore historical events or scientific concepts in an engaging, non-linear fashion.

Today's visual novels carry on this legacy, providing a powerful medium for narrative-driven instruction, empathy-building exercises, and the exploration of complex social and ethical dilemmas.

Why Visual Novels? An Economic Standpoint

For educators and institutions looking to create interactive content, visual novels present a compelling case from a resource perspective. Compared to fully-animated or 3D-rendered games, VNs are significantly more economical to produce.

  • Lower Art Asset Requirements: VNs primarily rely on character sprites, backgrounds, and UI elements. This is far less intensive than creating animated characters or complex 3D environments.
  • Focus on Writing: The core of a VN is its narrative. This allows faculty to leverage their subject matter expertise and focus on creating strong, impactful content without needing extensive technical skills.
  • Accessible Tools: Free and low-cost software like Ren'Py, Twine, and TyranoBuilder lower the barrier to entry, making VN development possible for individuals and small teams without a large budget.
  • Faster Development Cycle: The streamlined nature of VN creation allows for quicker iteration and development, meaning educational content can be produced and deployed more rapidly.

The Power of Immersion

Visual novels excel at immersing players in a story and putting them in the shoes of a character. By presenting players with choices, VNs make them active participants rather than passive observers. This agency fosters a deeper connection to the material.

For example, a game like Bury Me, My Love uses a familiar text-messaging interface to create a powerful, intimate connection to the refugee experience. Depression Quest effectively simulates the limited choices faced by someone with mental illness, building empathy through mechanics. These examples show that immersion isn't about graphical fidelity, but about meaningful interaction and perspective-taking.

Guide on Writing for Visual Novels

Writing for a visual novel is different from linear writing. It's about designing a character's journey that the player influences. A powerful framework for structuring this journey involves creating opportunities for the player to understand, challenge, and ultimately catalyze change in a character. This can be broken down into key stages.

1. Establish: Define the Character's Role

This is the introduction. You must clearly and efficiently establish who the character is, what they want, and what their core flaw or worldview is. This is the baseline from which all change will be measured. The player's relationship to this character is also defined here—are they a friend, a mentor, an adversary?

Example: A brilliant but arrogant medical student is introduced. They believe their intelligence is all that matters, and they dismiss the "soft skills" of patient communication. The player is their assigned partner for a project.

2. Enforce: Cement the Player's Conception

Reinforce the established traits through action and dialogue. Show, don't just tell. This stage solidifies the player's understanding of the character, making future changes more impactful. These early interactions should demonstrate the character's established worldview in practice.

Example: The medical student character bluntly delivers a difficult diagnosis to a simulated patient, focusing only on the technical data and ignoring their emotional distress, leading to a poor evaluation from the supervising doctor.

Inconsequential Player Interaction

During the "Establish" and "Enforce" stages, player choices should be largely inconsequential to the main plot. These early choices serve to build the player's own role and perspective. They allow the player to express their opinion about the character's behavior without fundamentally changing it yet. This makes the character feel more real and independent, rather than a puppet waiting for player input.

Example: The player can choose to agree with the arrogant student ("The data is what matters"), gently challenge them ("Maybe we should consider their feelings?"), or stay silent. Regardless of the choice, the student dismisses the feedback and proceeds with their established behavior. The choice is for the player's benefit, not the character's... yet.

3. Expose: Show the Character in a State of Vulnerability

This is the turning point. Create a situation where the character's established worldview fails them. They are confronted with the negative consequences of their flaw. This moment of vulnerability is crucial; it's the crack in their armor that makes them open to change.

Example: The medical student's own family member becomes ill. Their technical knowledge is useless in providing comfort, and they are unable to emotionally support their loved one, causing a rift between them. They feel helpless for the first time.

Consequential Player Interaction

This is where the player's agency becomes critical. Following the "Expose" stage, the character is now receptive to outside influence. The player's choice is no longer just an expression of opinion; it is the catalyst that pushes the character toward growth. This is the most important choice in the character's arc.

4. Player as Catalyst: The Consequential Choice

Present the player with a meaningful choice that directly addresses the character's moment of vulnerability. The player's action (or inaction) will set the character on a new path.

Example: Seeing the student struggling, the player can choose to: A) Share their own story of a similar personal struggle, modeling empathy. B) Offer practical advice on finding a counselor or support group. C) Give them space, respecting their need to process alone. Each choice validates a different approach to handling the emotional crisis.

5. Change: The Character is Different

Show the result of the player's catalytic choice. The character should now act in a way that is measurably different from their behavior in the "Enforce" stage. This new behavior should be a direct result of the player's intervention and the character's own reflection. This final stage validates the player's journey and makes their choices feel meaningful.

Example: In a final simulation, the student now handles a difficult conversation with a patient. They still present the technical data, but they do so with newfound empathy, pausing to listen and offer comfort—a behavior they learned because of the player's intervention.

Understanding Branching Paths

The core mechanic of a visual novel is the branching path. Unlike a linear story, a branching narrative presents the player with choices that alter the direction of the plot, leading to different scenes, character interactions, and endings. This structure is what gives the player a sense of agency and makes their decisions feel important.

Visually, a branching path can be simple or incredibly complex. A single choice can lead to a small variation in dialogue, or it can send the entire story in a completely new direction. Mapping out these branches is a crucial step in the design process.

Do different paths explore different facets of a topic? Do they demonstrate different consequences of a decision? Planning your branches helps ensure that every path provides a valuable learning experience.
